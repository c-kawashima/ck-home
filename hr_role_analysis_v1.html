Viewing: 1st Person — Chief of Staff lens
1 Short Narrative

I can see why this role might make sense on paper, and I believe I could add value in it. My concern is less about willingness or effort and more about whether the setup would be structurally winnable. If I am expected to help drive outcomes, raise quality, and improve execution, but the formal authority, organizational control, and pace of execution all still sit elsewhere, then there is a real risk that I become responsible for progress without fully controlling the mechanism required to create it.

What worries me most is not hard work. Hard work is fine. What worries me is being placed into a role where a great deal of energy is required just to compensate for structural ambiguity. In that kind of setup, it becomes difficult to create visible, sustainable traction. The risk is not only frustration in the short term. The risk is that over time the role becomes more about absorbing pressure, smoothing over contradictions, and supporting a constrained system than about building clear impact. That is why, from my perspective, the question is not whether I can work hard enough. The question is whether the setup allows my effort to convert into meaningful and legible progress.

1 Short Narrative: Issue, Concern, What to Do

The central concern is not whether the individual has capability or potential. The central concern is whether someone who could contribute meaningfully to both near-term and potentially longer-term organizational solutions is being placed into a setup that is structurally winnable. If the role is positioned in a way where expectations are high, but formal authority, organizational control, and pace of execution remain elsewhere, then even a strong person may struggle to convert effort into visible progress.

What makes this especially sensitive is the timing and the operating window. If too much time passes inside an ambiguous or partially constrained structure, the role can become frustrating and energy-consuming before any larger organizational decision is made. That creates a real risk that the individual becomes a compensating mechanism inside the system rather than someone whose impact is clearly recognized. The practical answer is to treat this as an operating-design question from the start: define visible ownership, clarify decision rights and escalation paths early, create short-interval review points, and make early impact legible so the system does not absorb effort without producing enough clarity for the next decision.

7 Appendix: Structured Walkthrough
A. Core message

The central concern is how an individual with real capability and potential to contribute to both near-term and potentially longer-term organizational solutions is placed into the right setup.

The issue is not mainly whether the individual is capable. The issue is whether the role is designed in a way that is structurally winnable.

If the setup separates expectations from control, or places the person inside a system with constrained authority and unclear decision rights, then even a strong individual may struggle to convert effort into visible and sustainable progress.

B. Context: the question is setup, not motive

This should not be framed as a question of motive or intent.

The more useful framing is:

  • there is a near-term organizational problem to solve
  • this role may also carry broader significance over time
  • the person being placed into it may be capable of contributing materially
  • but the design of the initial arrangement matters greatly

The concern is that if the role is introduced too quickly, with too much ambiguity, or inside a partially constrained structure, the person may spend a long period compensating for the system rather than building clear, visible traction.

So the question is not 'why is this being done.' The question is whether the setup gives the role a fair chance to succeed.

C. The pattern underneath

A recurring risk in these situations is that roles can make conceptual sense from the top but prove much harder to execute in the actual organization.

That tends to happen when:

  • formal reporting lines are assumed to solve practical control issues
  • the informal authority structure is underweighted
  • strong people are expected to work through unresolved contradictions
  • structures are changed quickly without enough protected transition time
  • ambiguity is tolerated longer than the role can healthily sustain

The result is that visible effort increases, but clear ownership and durable output do not increase at the same rate.

D. Why this specific situation is risky

The likely risk is not simply that the person fails. The more subtle risk is that the person works hard, improves things somewhat, but does so inside a structure that remains ambiguous.

That creates three possible scenarios:

Scenario 1: current leader fails clearly
If the current HR leader performs poorly and the contrast becomes obvious, the individual in the role may benefit because her capability becomes visible.
Scenario 2: current leader performs extremely well
If the current HR leader becomes highly successful with her support, the individual may also benefit because her contribution is legible and associated with the improvement.
Scenario 3: the middle ground
This is the dangerous one. If the person adds enough energy to improve outcomes somewhat, but not enough to resolve the structural issue, then the organization may tolerate the current arrangement longer. The system improves just enough to delay decisions. The structural contradictions remain. The person expends large amounts of effort. But the result is neither clear failure nor clear success. That is the lose-lose zone. This middle-ground outcome is the most dangerous because it can consume energy, blur credit, prolong uncertainty, and create burnout without producing enough clarity for action.
E. The timing risk

Timing is a major part of the concern.

If the role begins around early April and meaningful organizational decisions are not likely until October or November, that leaves a long period in which the person could be operating inside uncertainty.

That creates several risks:

  • too long a runway of structural ambiguity
  • too much time absorbing pressure without clean authority
  • fatigue building before larger decisions are made
  • frustration from seeing the issue clearly but not being able to resolve it cleanly
  • a risk that the role becomes emotionally expensive before it becomes organizationally rewarding

In other words: the duration of ambiguity matters almost as much as the design itself.

A role that is tolerable for eight weeks can become corrosive over six or seven months.

F. The structural contradiction

The individual may be expected to improve outcomes, raise quality, and add rigor.

But the existing HR leader may still hold:

  • formal authority
  • organizational hierarchy
  • practical resource control
  • day-to-day legitimacy with the team
  • influence over pace and process

That means:

  • expectations may rise quickly
  • visible control may not
  • credit may blur
  • friction may increase
  • escalation may become difficult to manage

When accountability rises faster than control, the person in the middle becomes the absorber of pressure.

G. Likely practical failure modes

If the role remains underdefined or structurally constrained, likely issues include:

  • the person being pulled into preparation and support work rather than direct impact
  • the existing leader using the role to increase output without changing control
  • repeated disagreements on priorities, pace, or quality
  • the broader team defaulting to formal hierarchy
  • visible effort not converting into visible ownership
  • too much energy being spent to produce only modest systemic movement
  • prolonged uncertainty leading to fatigue and resentment

This is how a strong person becomes trapped in system compensation rather than system leadership.

H. The real question

The real question is not: 'Can she handle it?'

The real question is: 'Is the setup designed so that her contribution becomes visible, sustainable, and structurally winnable?'

That is the right question.

8 Full Solutions Appendix
1. Define specific areas of direct ownership

Give the individual a few visible domains where ownership is explicit, not borrowed.

Examples could include:

  • specific strategic HR initiatives
  • direct presentation responsibility on selected topics
  • defined decision rights on certain operating items
  • ownership of synthesis and recommendations in specified forums

Why:

  • visible ownership makes contribution legible
  • clear wins reduce the middle-ground trap
  • it prevents the person from becoming merely an amplifier for someone else's authority
2. Clarify escalation and decision rights early

Do not leave the conflict-resolution mechanism vague.

Questions to answer upfront:

  • what happens when she and the HR leader disagree?
  • what decisions can she make directly?
  • what requires alignment?
  • when should issues be escalated?
  • what should not require escalation?

Why:

  • unresolved split-control is where frustration breeds
  • unclear rules force politics into every decision
3. Create short-interval review points

Instead of waiting until autumn to assess whether the setup is working, create explicit check-ins much earlier.

For example: 30 days, 60 days, 90 days.

Why:

  • it reduces the risk of drift
  • it surfaces structural problems before fatigue hardens
  • it gives the CEO clean points to reassess without waiting for a crisis
4. Make early wins visible and attributable

This is critical.

If the person improves the system, that improvement must be visible enough that it does not just disappear into the background and preserve the status quo.

Why:

  • otherwise the middle ground wins
  • the structure gets tolerated because things are 'a bit better'
  • the person spends energy but does not accumulate enough recognized traction

Useful framing: If impact is real, it should be legible early.

5. Shorten the ambiguity window

If the role is going to be created quickly, then the period of unclear structure should be deliberately shortened.

Why:

  • ambiguity compounds fatigue
  • long transition periods trap people in compensation mode
  • uncertainty from April through October or November is a long time in a politically constrained role

Useful framing: If the structure cannot be fully resolved now, the duration of ambiguity should still be contained.

6. Protect the role from becoming pure support labor

If the role becomes mostly preparation, coordination, or compensation for the existing leader's weaknesses, the setup will drain the person quickly.

Why:

  • support work can be endless
  • it creates dependence without authority
  • it makes the contribution less visible
  • it erodes the strategic value of the role

Useful line: The role should not become a buffer for structural inefficiency.
